The IS-LM Model

2019/01/10

A simple IS-LM model for an open economy can be defined as:

\[ \begin{align} Y &= C(Y, T(Y)) + I(Y, i) + G + X(Y^W, e(i, i^W)) - M(Y, e(i, i^W)) \\ M/P &= L(Y, i) \\ -K(i, i^W) &= X(Y^W, e(i, i^W)) - M(Y, e(i, i^W)) \\ E &= e(i, i^W) \end{align} \] in which \(Y\) is the local GDP; \(C\) is a consumption function that depends on the \(Y\) and \(T\) (taxes); \(I\) is the investment function that depends on \(Y\) and \(i\) (interest-rates). The goods market is connected with the rest of the world thought \(X\) and \(M\). Both depend on \(e\) (the real exchange rate), but \(X\) grows with \(Y^W\) (the growth in the rest of the world) while \(M\) has a positive association with \(Y\), the domestic product. Since the focus is the short-term dynamics, the price level (\(P\)) is given as a constant.

In this model the endogenous variables are \(Y\), \(i\), \(e\) while \(G\), \(M/P\), \(i^*\) and \(Y^*\) are exogenous.

The partial derivatives for the goods market are deffined as follows:

  • \(C_Y > 0\): an increase in \(Y\) boosts consumption;
  • \(C_T < 0\): an increase in \(T\) decreases consumption;
  • \(I_Y > 0\): an increase in \(Y\) increases investment;
  • \(I_i < 0\): an increase in \(i\) decreases investment;
  • \(X_{Y^W} > 0\) if the global GDP grows, so do the exports;
  • \(X_e < 0\): a apreciation in \(e\) makes the domestic products less atractive in the foreigner eyes;
  • \(X_e e_i < 0\): an increase in \(i\) treagers an apreciation in \(e\) that decreases \(X\);
  • \(X_e e_{i^W} > 0\) an increase in \(i^W\) depreciatiates \(e\) and boosts \(X\);
  • \(M_Y > 0\) when the the domestic GDP grows a liking is created thoguht imports;
  • \(M_e > 0\): a apreciation in \(e\) makes the domestic products more atractive in the domestic country;
  • \(M_e e_i M > 0\): an increase in \(i\) treagers an apreciation in \(e\) that increases \(M\);
  • \(M_e e_{i^W} < 0\) an increase in \(i^W\) depreciatiates \(e\) and reduce \(M\);

The financial markets (represented by \(M/P\)), on the other hand, respond to the following instantaneous changes:

  • \((M/P)_Y >0\): an increase in \(Y\) also increases the demand for money;
  • \((M/P)_i < 0\): when interest-rates rise, the relative cost of holding money goes up and people keep a smaller amount of money in their pockets.

Declivity of \(Y\), \(i\) and \(e\)

Knowing the relationship that exogenous and endogenous variables have with each other, it is possible to uncover the slope of \(Y\), \(i\) and \(e\) in the \(y-i\) plane.

Differentiate the first equation (this is the IS curve) in respect to all variables to get

\[ dY = [C_Y - T_Y + I_Y - M_Y]dY + [-I_i - X_e e_i + M_e e_i]di \] Reorganizing

\[ [1 - C_Y + T_Y - I_Y + M_Y]dY = [-I_i - (X_e e_i - M_e e_i)]di \] and dividing both sides by \([1 - C_Y + T_Y - I_Y + M_Y]di\) its possible to find

\[ \frac{dY}{di} = - \frac{I_i + (X_e e_i - M_e e_i)}{1 - C_Y + T_Y - I_Y + M_Y} < 0 \] Given the partials defined above, the goods market have a negative slope in the \(Y-i\) hyperplane.

For the financial markets, \(M/P = L(Y, i)\), it’s easy to show that

\[ L_y dY + Li di = 0 \]

which is the same as

\[ \frac{dY}{di} = - \frac{L_i}{L_Y} > 0 \] Thus, financial markets show a positive association between \(Y-i\).

Finally, differentiating \(e = E(i, i^W)\) with respect to \(Y\) and \(i\) results in

\[ dY = e_i di \\ \frac{dY}{di} = e_i > 0 \]

An increment in the interest rates makes domestic money market more attractive in relation to international funds industry. Assuming investors are risk-neutral, this difference puts an upper pressure in exchange rates. The change stops when the demand and supply for funds clear. Thus, the uncovered interest rate parity shows a positive slope in the \(Y-i\) hyperplane.

Is there a solution for this problem?

To anwser this question the system has to be witten as equilíbrium identities

\[ \begin{bmatrix} Y - C(Y, T(Y)) - I(Y, i) - G - X(Y^W, e(i, i^W)) + M(Y, e(i, i^*)) \\ L(Y, i) - M/P \\ X(Y^W, e(i, i^W)) - M(Y, e(i, i^*)) + K(i, i^W) \end{bmatrix} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Taking a complete diferentiation results in

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y} & I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i & X_e + M_e \\ L_{Y} & -L_{i} & 0 \\ -M_{Y} & 0 & -(X_e + M_e) \end{bmatrix} \neq 0 \] This is a standard Jacobean Matrix. If the resulting determinant is different than zero we can be preatty confident that the whole system offers a unique solution.

Before proceding let me enphasize that the partials in the \(a_{2,3}\) position are set to zero because the flows in the current account just balance the flows in the capital account, with the opposite sign. Taking this step here will further easy the calculations.

Continuing with the determinant

\[ \big[(1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y}) L_i (X_e + M_e) \big] - \big[ (X_e + M_e) L_i M_Y - (X_e + M_e)(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i) L_Y \big] \]

Colecting \((X_e + M_e)\)

\[ \underbrace{(X_e + M_e)}_{-} \Bigg[ \underbrace{ (1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} )}_{+} \underbrace{ L_i}_{-} + \underbrace{(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i)}_{-} \underbrace{L_Y}_{+} \Bigg] \]

If we assume that Marshall–Lerner condition holds, that is, the balance of trade does not sum to zero, the determinant is positive and it is possible to express the implicit functions around it’s equilibrium values

\[ \begin{align} Y^* &= Y^*(G, M/P, r^w, Y^w) \\ i^* &= i^*(G, M/P, r^w, Y^w) \\ e^* &= e^*(G, M/P, r^w, Y^w) \end{align} \] This system can be written in matrix format as \(Ax=d\). \(A\) is the Jacobian Matrix (calculated above), \(x\) contains the endogenus variables around its equilibrium values and \(d\) is composed of exogenous shocks.

since \(det(A) \neq 0\) its possible to writte the solution as \(x=A^{-1}d\).

In matrix format after a complete differentiation yields

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y} & I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i & X_e + M_e \\ L_{Y} & -L_{i} & 0 \\ -M_{Y} & 0 & -(X_e + M_e) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} dY^* \\ di^* \\ de^* \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} dG + X_{Y_{W}}dY_W - (X_e e_{i_{W}} - M_e e_{i_{W}} ) d_{i_{W}} \\ d(M/P) \\ X_{Y_{W}}dY_W + (X_e e_{i_{W}} - M_e e_{i_{W}} - K_{i_{W}}) d_{i_{W}} \end{bmatrix} \]

\[ \begin{bmatrix} dY^* \\ di^* \\ de^* \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{det} \begin{bmatrix} (X_e + M_e)L_i & (X_e + M_e)L_Y & L_i M_Y \\ (X_e + M_e)(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i) & -(X_e + M_e) \big[1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} \big] & -M_Y(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i) \\ (X_e + M_e)L_i & (X_e + M_e)L_Y & -(1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y})L_i - (I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i)L_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} dG + X_{Y_{W}}dY_W - (X_e e_{i_{W}} - M_e e_{i_{W}} ) d_{i_{W}} \\ d(M/P) \\ X_{Y_{W}}dY_W + (X_e e_{i_{W}} - M_e e_{i_{W}} - K_{i_{W}}) d_{i_{W}} \end{bmatrix} \] This give us a very complicated expression. Fortunatly it can be greatly simplified once we specify the source of exógenous shocks. For example, to test for a change around the equilibrium from a given shock in \(M/P\) the system simplifies to

\[ \begin{bmatrix} dY^* \\ di^* \\ de^* \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{det} \begin{bmatrix} (X_e + M_e)L_i & (X_e + M_e)L_Y & L_i M_Y \\ (X_e + M_e)(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i) & (X_e + M_e) \big[M_Y - (1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y}) \big] & -M_Y(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i) \\ (X_e + M_e)L_i & (X_e + M_e)L_Y & -(1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y})L_i - (I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i)L_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ d(M/P) \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

which lead us to

\[ \begin{bmatrix} dY^* \\ di^* \\ de^* \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{det} \begin{bmatrix} (X_e + M_e)L_Y \\ (X_e + M_e) \big[M_Y - (1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y} + M_{Y}) \big] \\ (X_e + M_e)L_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ d(M/P) \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \] Dividing both sides by \(d(M/P)\)

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \frac{dY^*}{d(M/P)} \\ \frac{di^*}{d(M/P)} \\ \frac{de^*}{d(M/P)} \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{(1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y})L_i - \big(I_i + X_e e_i + M_e e_i \big) L_Y} \begin{bmatrix} L_Y \\ -(1 - C_{Y} - C_{Y}T_{Y} - I_{Y}) \\ L_Y \end{bmatrix} \]
